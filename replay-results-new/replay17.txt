## Why the Gap Exists (high-confidence drivers)

1. **Labor law structure**: Mexico's legal workweek is 48 hours vs. Canada's ~40 hours under most provincial/federal standards
2. **Informal economy**: ~55-57% of Mexican workers operate informally (INEGI data) with no enforced hour limits, vs. Canada's predominantly formal workforce
3. **Productivity differential**: Canada produces roughly 2-3x more GDP per hour worked, meaning fewer hours generate equivalent economic value
4. **Vacation/holidays**: Mexico reformed its minimum to 12 days in 2023 (up from 6); Canada's statutory minimums are 2-3 weeks depending on province

## What Can Go Wrong With This Data

1. **OECD methodology isn't perfectly comparable** across countries (different national surveys, different estimation methods for informal work) ‚Äî no proposal mentioned this
2. **Averages hide enormous within-country variance** ‚Äî a Monterrey manufacturing worker and a Oaxacan farmer live in different labor universes
3. **Gender composition**: Female labor force participation differs significantly (~60% Canada, ~45% Mexico), changing who the "average worker" is
4. **Informal sector paradox**: Mexico's real hours may be *higher* than reported, making the gap *larger* than OECD shows

## Bottom Line

The ~25-30% gap in annual hours worked is **structural**, driven by economic development stage, labor law, informality, and productivity differences ‚Äî not individual work ethic. Hours worked is a poor proxy for economic value; Canada produces far more output per hour. Any model, policy, or business assumption treating labor inputs as equivalent between these countries contains a critical flaw.
