## What About US Practice vs. Other Countries' Minimums?

US workers *average* roughly 10-15 days after one year of service (per BLS Employee Benefits Survey data). This means the *average* American worker roughly matches the *legal floor* of Canada ‚Äî the lowest Western mandate. But ~21-23% of US private-sector workers receive **zero** paid vacation. Comparing US averages to foreign statutory minimums is an apples-to-oranges error that overstates American generosity.

## What Can Go Wrong With This Analysis

1. **Enforcement gap ignored:** All models treated statutory minimums as effective guarantees. In practice, Southern European informal economies and gig workers everywhere may not receive mandated leave.
2. **Gig/contractor exclusion:** Statutory minimums typically cover *employees* only. As gig work grows globally, effective coverage shrinks ‚Äî including in Europe.
3. **No verifiable primary sources:** No model provided specific statute citations or report page numbers. "OECD 2023" was cited repeatedly but likely does not contain the exact tables presented. Treat specific numbers as approximately correct, not precisely sourced.
4. **US trajectory:** Several US states now mandate paid sick leave; paid vacation mandates may follow. The static picture is accurate today but evolving.

## Bottom Line

The US stands alone among Western nations in guaranteeing zero paid vacation. Even the most conservative Western mandate (Canada's 10 days) is infinitely more than the US federal floor. Comparing US employer *averages* to foreign *legal minimums* is methodologically flawed and misleading for policy, HR, or competitive analysis.
