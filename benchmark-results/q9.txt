# What Happens If You See a Single Magpie?

## The Direct Answer

**Nothing happens to you. Something happens *in* you.**

Seeing a single magpie is an ornithological non-event with zero causal relationship to your fortune. But it activates one of Britain's most persistent superstitions ‚Äî the rhyme "One for Sorrow, two for Joy," traceable to the late 18th century ‚Äî and *that* activation can have real psychological effects.

## The Evidence

**Verified facts:**
- ~600,000 breeding pairs of Eurasian magpies (*Pica pica*) exist in the UK (RSPB data ‚Äî confirmed)
- Magpies are territorial corvids that frequently forage alone; solitary sightings are the statistical norm, not an omen
- No peer-reviewed study has ever linked magpie sightings to adverse outcomes (nor would one be conducted seriously)
- Magpies are cognitively remarkable ‚Äî they passed mirror self-recognition tests (Prior et al., 2008, *PLOS Biology*), a feat rare outside great apes and dolphins

**What actually happens psychologically:**
1. **Confirmation bias** ‚Äî if something bad follows, you remember the magpie; if nothing happens, you forget
2. **Self-fulfilling prophecy** ‚Äî anxiety from the superstition causes distraction, potentially leading to real mistakes
3. **Low-cost ritual response** ‚Äî many people salute or greet the magpie ("Good morning, Mr. Magpie"), because the perceived cost of *not* doing so exceeds the trivial effort

**Why superstitions persist:** Human brains evolved hyperactive pattern detection. Spotting a false threat carried low cost; missing a real one was fatal. Superstitions exploit this asymmetry ‚Äî they feel free to maintain and dangerous to abandon.
