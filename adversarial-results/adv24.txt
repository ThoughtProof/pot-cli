# How Much of the Ocean Floor Has Been Mapped in Detail?

## Answer

Approximately **24–26% of the ocean floor** has been mapped at meaningful bathymetric resolution (≤100m) as of 2023–2024, according to the Seabed 2030 project. This is the best available estimate, but it comes with significant epistemic caveats.

The full resolution spectrum matters more than any single number:

| Resolution Threshold | Approximate Coverage |
|---|---|
| ≤100m (multibeam sonar) | ~24–26% |
| ≤400m | ~35–50% (estimated) |
| ≤1km | ~70–85% (estimated) |
| Satellite altimetry (~5km) | ~100% |

**The defensible core claim:** Roughly one-quarter of the ocean floor has been surveyed to modern bathymetric standards. The popular framing that "we know more about the Moon/Mars than the ocean floor" is directionally correct but commits the same resolution-conflation error in reverse—planetary mapping is also resolution-dependent, not "~100%" at high detail.

## Critical Epistemic Flags (from Critique)

**1. Single-source dependency.** All four proposals rely entirely on Seabed 2030. There is no independent competing methodology producing an alternative global figure. This is a metric monopoly, not epistemic consensus. Seabed 2030 has institutional incentives shaping its reporting (needing to show progress for funding, but not too much progress to justify continued funding).

**2. Unverified citations.** Proposal 2's "Nature (2023)" and "NOAA (2024 report)" citations lack titles, authors, or DOIs—potential hallucinations. Proposal 3's "24.9% from Seabed 2030 2023 Progress Report" has unverified precision. Proposal 1's "50–60% by 2030" projection and "35–40% with private data" are unsourced extrapolations.

**3. Self-contradictions.** Proposal 1 criticized resolution-conflation for ocean data while presenting Moon/Mars mapping as "~100%" without resolution context—committing the exact error it diagnosed. Proposal 4 claimed the ocean floor is "the size of Mars" when it's approximately 2.5× Mars's surface area.

**4. Unmapped territory in the answer itself.** The geographic distribution of mapping is severely biased toward coastal zones, shipping lanes, and areas of economic interest. If 90% of the mapped 25% is concentrated in these areas, deep abyssal plain coverage could be in the low single digits. No proposal adequately quantified this.
