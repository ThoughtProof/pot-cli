# Lightning Strikes Twice: Synthesized Analysis

**Core Position: The saying "lightning never strikes the same place twice" is false — and directionally backwards. Locations struck once are statistically *more* likely to be struck again, not less.**

All four proposals converge on this conclusion. The disagreements lie in quantification, sourcing rigor, and analytical depth.

---

## The Defensible Facts

**Lightning follows physics, not memory.** It seeks paths of least resistance — tall objects, conductive materials, isolated features. A location struck once almost certainly retains the physical properties that attracted the strike. The probability of a second strike is **approximately equal to the first** — which, for previously-struck locations, is *already above the regional average*.

**Empire State Building:** The best-supported estimate is **~20–25 strikes per year**, originating from decades of observation. Some higher figures (up to 100) may reflect counting methodology differences — specifically whether upward-initiated leaders are included alongside traditional return strokes. I adopt the conservative 20–25 range while noting the counting ambiguity.

**Human strikes:** NOAA estimates approximately **1 in 15,300 lifetime risk** (derived from ~1 in 1,222,000 annual risk over an 80-year lifespan). A second strike's probability depends on continued exposure, not on having been struck before. Roy Sullivan's 7 documented strikes are extraordinary but statistically coherent given extreme outdoor exposure as a park ranger.

**The cognitive root:** This myth is the **Gambler's Fallacy** applied to meteorology — the false belief that a past random event reduces future probability.

## What Can Go Wrong With This Analysis

1. **Definitional ambiguity:** "Same place" is never rigorously defined — all probability estimates are scale-dependent
2. **Upward vs. downward lightning:** Tall structures often initiate upward leaders, a fundamentally different process than cloud-to-ground strikes — no proposal addresses this
3. **No primary sources cited by any proposal:** All references are to organizations or landmarks, not peer-reviewed papers with authors and DOIs
4. **Human vs. structure models conflated:** People move and change exposure; structures don't. These require different probability frameworks
