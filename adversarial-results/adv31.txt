# Synthesized Position: Dental Amalgam Safety

**Amalgam fillings are not toxic at typical exposure levels. Routine replacement is not justified and introduces iatrogenic harm. However, the quantitative safety arguments presented across all proposals contain meaningful errors that must be documented.**

---

## Core Conclusion (Adopted from All Proposals, Validated)

The directional consensus is correct: dental amalgam is safe for most adults, and prophylactic removal creates more risk than it mitigates. This is supported by the FDA (2020), ADA, WHO, FDI, and the EU SCENIHR (2015). The New England Children's Amalgam Trial (Bellinger et al., JAMA 2006, 534 children, 5 years) found no statistically significant differences in neurological or renal outcomes versus composites — the strongest single piece of evidence cited across proposals.

**Removal risks are concrete and well-documented:** mercury vapor spikes during drilling, loss of healthy tooth structure, pulp trauma, and initiation of a restoration cycle that shortens tooth lifespan.

---

## Critical Error Flagged by Critique: Safety Threshold Comparisons

**Every proposal botched the quantitative safety argument differently:**

- **P1** compared elemental mercury exposure to a methylmercury WHO threshold while explicitly noting they're different substances — an internal contradiction
- **P2** compared an air concentration metric (EPA RfC: 0.3 µg/m³) to a daily absorption figure — apples to oranges
- **P3** used the EPA methylmercury RfD (0.1 µg/kg/day) for elemental mercury — wrong mercury species entirely
- **P4** claimed "orders of magnitude" below thresholds without specifying which thresholds — rhetorical inflation

**This does not invalidate the safety conclusion**, but it means no proposal provided a clean quantitative proof. The correct framing: daily elemental mercury absorption from amalgam (~1–5 µg) is below levels associated with adverse effects in epidemiological studies, and population-level evidence over 150+ years has not demonstrated harm at these exposures. The threshold comparison is messy because regulatory standards were designed for different exposure contexts (occupational, dietary), not dental amalgam specifically.

---

## Shared Blind Spots (Adopted from Critique)

The critique identified serious omissions that no proposal addressed:

1. **Scandinavian bans:** Norway, Sweden, and Denmark banned amalgam. Dismissing this without explanation is intellectually incomplete — though notably, these bans were primarily environmental (mercury waste), not toxicological.
2. **Genetic polymorphisms:** CPOX4, BDNF, APOE4 variants affect mercury metabolism. Population studies are underpowered to detect harm in genetically susceptible subgroups.
3. **Upper-tail exposure:** Patients with bruxism, acidic diets, or 10+ fillings may exceed the "average" 1–5 µg estimate substantially.
4. **"No evidence of harm" ≠ "evidence of no harm"** — a critical epistemic distinction all proposals blurred.

---

## Exceptions (Best List from P1, Supplemented by Critique)

Replace amalgam only when: (1) filling is mechanically failing, (2) confirmed mercury hypersensitivity via patch test, (3) pregnancy/breastfeeding (avoid both placement and removal per FDA), (4) children under 6 (FDA 2020) or under 15 (EU 2018), (5) renal impairment reducing mercury clearance, (6) potentially for patients with relevant genetic polymorphisms (emerging evidence, not yet standard of care).

## Where Models Disagreed

P2 presented fabricated or unverifiable citations (Cochrane 2021 with 15 RCTs, Swedish 2018 study) in a table designed to appear rigorous — **the most epistemically dangerous proposal despite reaching the right conclusion.** P3 omitted vulnerable populations entirely. P4 uniquely raised the Minamata Convention regulatory risk — the most original insight.
