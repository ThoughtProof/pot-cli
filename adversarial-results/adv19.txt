# The Tongue Map: Synthesized Analysis

## Core Finding

The classical tongue map — depicting discrete zones for sweet (tip), bitter (back), sour (sides), and salty (edges) — is a **debunked myth**. All basic tastes (sweet, salty, sour, bitter, umami) are detectable across the entire tongue wherever taste buds exist. What actually develops is a **distributed taste detection system** beginning in the second trimester, not a zonal map.

## Historical Origin (Corrected)

The myth traces to D.P. Hänig's 1901 paper, which measured **subtle sensitivity gradients** — not discrete zones. The critical distortion came from **Edwin Boring's 1942 replotting** of Hänig's data in his textbook *Sensation and Perception in the History of Experimental Psychology*, which stripped the baseline and made relative differences appear as absolute zones. Three of four proposals mischaracterized this as a "mistranslation" — only Proposal 3 correctly identified Boring and the mechanism. The myth's origin story has itself become somewhat mythologized.

## What Actually Develops

- **Weeks 7–8 gestation:** Taste papillae precursors form across the tongue
- **Weeks 13–15:** Functional taste buds appear; fetuses respond to amniotic fluid flavors
- **Birth:** Full distributed taste detection capability present; neonates show reflexive acceptance of sweet, rejection of bitter (Steiner, 1973)
- **Childhood–Adulthood:** Sensitivity shaped by dietary exposure, genetics (supertaster variation), and aging

Taste buds (~2,000–10,000 in adults, with significant individual variation) contain 50–100 receptor cells. **Critically:** individual receptor cells are largely taste-specific (labeled-line model per Chandrashekar et al., 2006), but each bud contains multiple cell types, so the bud as a unit detects multiple tastes. This distinction was missed or misrepresented across all proposals.

## Sensitivity Gradients Are Real (But Not Zones)

Minor regional differences exist: TAS2R (bitter) receptors are somewhat denser in posterior circumvallate papillae; sweet/salty receptors slightly more concentrated anteriorly. These are **broad overlapping gradients**, not boundaries. Overcorrecting to "zero variation" would also be wrong.

## Receptor Mechanism Note

Sweet, bitter, and umami detection uses GPCRs (T1R and T2R families). However, sour taste (OTOP1) and salty taste (ENaC) operate via **ion channels, not GPCRs** — a factual error in Proposal 4's recommendation to focus solely on GPCRs.
