## Core Finding

**All four proposals converge on the correct conclusion: the sugar-hyperactivity link is a well-debunked myth.** This consensus is grounded in real evidence, primarily the Wolraich et al. 1995 JAMA meta-analysis (verified) and the Hoover & Milich 1994 expectancy study (verified). The question contains a false premise, and the epistemically defensible answer must correct that premise before offering practical guidance.

## Synthesized Answer

### The science is clear but aging:
The controlled trial evidence (concentrated 1985-1995) consistently shows no causal sugar-hyperactivity link. The observed "sugar rush" is explained by **contextual excitement** (parties, holidays), **parental expectation bias** (confirmed in blinded designs), and **sleep/routine disruption**. The Wolraich meta-analysis conclusion was that sugar does not meaningfully affect behavior or cognition — though it noted a small effect on subsets "cannot be ruled out." That hedge matters and was only acknowledged by Proposal 1.

### What to actually manage:
- **Context and environment** — structure transitions after high-stimulation events
- **Sleep schedules** — the single most evidence-supported behavioral lever
- **Expectation bias** — informing caregivers of the evidence (while acknowledging belief change is hard)
- **Overall dietary quality** — for dental and metabolic health, not behavior control
- **Real conditions** — persistent behavioral issues warrant ADHD/anxiety screening, not sugar blame

### What goes wrong if you accept the myth:
- Real conditions (ADHD, anxiety, sleep disorders) get scapegoated
- Unnecessary food restriction creates family stress and potential disordered eating risk
- Institutional reinforcement of the myth delays evidence-based interventions

### Honest limitations the proposals share:
- The evidence base is **30 years old**; modern sugar consumption levels and HFCS prevalence differ from study conditions
- Individual variation (metabolic disorders, extreme consumption) is dismissed too quickly
- Sugar does affect mood and energy through glycemic variability — this isn't "hyperactivity" but it's what many parents actually observe and describe imprecisely
- None addressed the sucrose/fructose/glucose distinction relevant to contemporary diets
