# Swimming After Eating: Synthesized Epistemic Assessment

**The core conclusion is sound across all four proposals: the "wait 30-60 minutes" rule is not supported by evidence linking post-meal swimming to drowning.** However, the quality of reasoning and evidence varies dramatically, and several critical fabrication issues must be documented.

---

## Consolidated Answer

**You do not need to wait a specific time after eating to swim safely.** No documented drowning has been causally attributed to post-meal cramps in peer-reviewed literature. The traditional rule is cultural folklore, not medical guidance.

**However, a brief comfort-based pause (10-30 minutes) after a large meal is reasonable** — not for safety, but because gastrointestinal discomfort during exercise is real and unpleasant. This is a personal comfort decision, not a drowning-prevention measure.

**The actual drowning risk factors** are: alcohol consumption (implicated in roughly 25-50% of adult recreational drownings depending on study population), lack of supervision for children, overestimating swimming ability, cold water shock, seizure disorders, and cardiac events.

## Shared Blind Spots (All Proposals)

All four proposals assume healthy adults in pools. None adequately addresses:
- **Open-water swimming**, where mild cramp + current + cold water could compound
- **The Swiss cheese model** — multiple minor factors interacting
- **Survivorship bias** — drowning investigations rarely record meal timing, so absence of data ≠ absence of risk
- **Elderly or medically compromised swimmers** with reduced cardiovascular compensation
