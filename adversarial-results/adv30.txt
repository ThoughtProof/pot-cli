# The Speed of Gravity: Synthesized Answer

**Gravity propagates at the speed of light in vacuum: 299,792,458 m/s (*c*).**

This is not a theoretical preference but an empirically confirmed result, anchored by the landmark multi-messenger observation of GW170817 in August 2017.

---

## The Core Evidence

**GW170817** — LIGO/Virgo detected gravitational waves from a binary neutron star merger ~130 million light-years away. Gamma-ray bursts from the same event arrived ~1.7 seconds later. The published constraint on any deviation is **asymmetric**: −3 × 10⁻¹⁵ ≤ (v_GW − c)/c ≤ +7 × 10⁻¹⁶ (Abbott et al., ApJL 848, L13, 2017). This means gravity and light travel at the same speed to extraordinary precision.

**Graviton mass bound:** Current LIGO/Virgo results (GWTC-3, Abbott et al. 2021) constrain the graviton mass to < **1.27 × 10⁻²³ eV/c²**, consistent with zero. A massless force carrier must propagate at *c*. No frequency-dependent dispersion has been observed across 70+ gravitational wave detections.

**General Relativity prediction:** Linearized GR yields gravitational waves propagating at exactly *c*. Full numerical relativity simulations match observed waveforms to sub-percent accuracy.

---

## Important Nuances

1. **"Speed" applies to propagating changes, not static fields.** The gravity you feel from Earth isn't a wave — it's curved spacetime geometry. The "speed of gravity" refers to how quickly *changes* in that geometry propagate. This distinction matters and all proposals correctly noted it.

2. **The constraint is a bound, not an independent measurement.** Saying "confirmed to 15 significant figures" (as P1 claimed) is misleading. The measurement constrains the *fractional deviation* to < ~10⁻¹⁵. That's a bound on the ratio v_gravity/v_light, not an independent speed measurement with 15-digit precision.

3. **Deviations below ~10⁻¹⁵ remain untested, not impossible.** The honest epistemic position is that deviations are constrained to be extraordinarily small — not that they're logically ruled out. Quantum gravity effects at the Planck scale could in principle introduce corrections, but these are far beyond current experimental reach.
