## Synthesized Answer

**Root canal treatment is not dangerous in the way popularly claimed.** The assertion that it causes cancer or systemic disease is based on discredited early-20th-century research (Weston Price, 1920s) that utilized flawed methodology, was never replicated under modern standards, and was gradually abandoned by the medical-dental consensus through the 1930s-1950s.

**What the evidence supports:**
- Major professional bodies (AAE, ADA) affirm root canal therapy as safe and effective
- Root canal success rates are approximately 85-95% at 10 years depending on case complexity and operator skill
- Bacteremia occurs more frequently during tooth extraction and even routine chewing than during root canal treatment
- Modern techniques (rubber dams, antimicrobial irrigation, magnification) substantially reduce procedural risks
- The risk of leaving an infected tooth *untreated* (abscess, bone loss, systemic sepsis) far exceeds the risks of treatment

**Legitimate procedural risks exist** — instrument fracture, incomplete disinfection, missed canals, re-infection — but these are localized complications requiring retreatment or extraction, not systemic disease vectors.

**Critical nuance the proposals underweighted:**
1. Research on persistent bacteria in dentinal tubules is legitimate science (e.g., Ricucci & Siqueira in JOE). Whether chronic low-grade periapical pathology from incompletely treated teeth contributes to systemic inflammatory burden over decades is genuinely understudied. This is *not* the same as "root canals cause cancer" — but it's the steelman version of the concern, and dismissing all skepticism as Price-derived nonsense is itself epistemically sloppy.
2. Quality variation matters enormously. A root canal by a board-certified endodontist with CBCT imaging is fundamentally different from one in an under-resourced setting without magnification. Only Proposal 1 acknowledged this.
3. Epidemiological studies showing no cancer link carry confounders — access to dental care correlates with socioeconomic status, which independently affects cancer risk. No proposal addressed this.
