# Napoleon's Height and Military Strategy: Synthesized Analysis

## Core Finding

**Napoleon was not short. The question contains a false premise, and the epistemically defensible answer is to reject the frame rather than analyze a fiction.**

Napoleon stood approximately **5'6"–5'7" (169–170 cm)** — average to slightly above average for a French male of his era (estimated ~164–166 cm per Komlos's anthropometric studies). The "short Napoleon" myth derives from two documented sources: (1) a unit conversion error conflating longer French *pouces* with shorter British inches, and (2) British wartime propaganda caricatures, most famously by James Gillray (though not Gillray alone — the "Little Boney" trope was a broader cultural phenomenon).

Answering "how did his shortness affect strategy" would be **laundering a false premise into analysis** — producing confident-sounding nonsense from a defective foundation. This is precisely the epistemic failure mode a verification protocol should catch.

## What Actually Drove Napoleon's Strategy

His military innovations had documented, non-mythological causes:
- **Corps d'armée system** — self-contained units enabling independent movement and operational speed
- **Concentration of force** — massing at the enemy's weakest point, derived from his artillery background
- **Manœuvre sur les derrières** — cutting enemy lines of communication to force decisive engagement
- **Meritocratic promotion** — talent over aristocratic birth, improving command quality

None of these trace to physical stature.

## The Perception Question (Shared Blind Spot)

All proposals dismissed second-order effects too quickly. Even if Napoleon was average height, his enemies **believed and propagandized** him as short. Did caricatures cause foreign officers to underestimate him? Did the "Little Corporal" nickname (origin debated — affectionate term vs. rank reference) affect troop rapport? These are legitimate historical questions distinct from the false "short Napoleon drove strategy" claim. The honest answer: we have insufficient primary evidence to determine whether the *perception* of his height created real strategic dynamics, though the possibility cannot be categorically excluded.

## Where Models Disagreed

**They didn't disagree on substance** — all four correctly identified the myth and rejected the premise. The disagreements were in **rigor and reliability**:

- **Measurement precision**: Proposals varied between 168–170 cm without any citing primary sources directly. The autopsy measurement (Antommarchi, 1821) is the strongest evidence but is itself contested regarding which unit system was used.
- **"Le petit caporal"**: Proposal 3 called it "a term of affection"; Proposal 4 called it "a branding exercise." Neither interpretation is well-sourced; the origin remains debated among historians.
- **Average French male height**: Only Proposal 3 attempted a specific figure (~165 cm). This is the most important contextual number and it went largely unsubstantiated across proposals.
