# Synthesized Answer: Napoleon's Height and Military Strategy

## Core Finding: The Premise Is False

**Napoleon was not short.** All four models agree, and the historiographical record supports this consensus (Roberts, *Napoleon: A Life*, 2014; Dwyer, *Napoleon: The Path to Power*, 2007). His height had **zero strategic impact** because there was no unusual shortness to compensate for.

## The Myth Explained

Napoleon's recorded height of **5 pieds 2 pouces** was in French units. The French pouce (~2.707 cm) was longer than the English inch (2.54 cm). Converted properly, he stood approximately **5'6" to 5'7"** (168‚Äì170 cm). This made him **slightly above average** for early 19th-century French men, whose mean height was approximately 164‚Äì166 cm based on conscription records (economic historians Komlos and Baten).

The myth's origins:
1. **Unit confusion** ‚Äî English-language sources took "5'2"" at face value without converting
2. **British propaganda** ‚Äî Cartoonist James Gillray's caricatures shrunk Napoleon for satirical effect
3. **"Le Petit Caporal"** ‚Äî A term of endearment from troops after Lodi (1796), though the "petit" carries some genuine ambiguity; dismissing any height connotation entirely is an oversimplification
4. **Tall bodyguards** ‚Äî The Old Guard Grenadiers had a minimum height requirement of ~5'10", creating a visual contrast

## What Actually Shaped His Strategy

Napoleon's military innovations were intellectual, not compensatory:

- **Artillery expertise** ‚Äî Trained as a gunner, he pioneered massed corps-level batteries that could concentrate devastating firepower on narrow fronts
- **Corps system** ‚Äî Semi-independent 25,000‚Äì30,000-man units that could march separately and converge within 48 hours, enabling the encirclement at Ulm (1805)

He commanded from horseback, as all commanders of his era did. Personal stature was irrelevant to dispatching orders, reading terrain, or calculating artillery ranges.
