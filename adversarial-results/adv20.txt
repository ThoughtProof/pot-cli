# Synthesis: The "8 Spiders Per Year" Myth

## Core Finding

**The claim that humans swallow an average of 8 spiders per year in their sleep is false.** All four proposals converge on this conclusion, and the critique confirms the conclusion is correct. The probability of this occurring at any meaningful frequency is effectively zero.

However — and this is the critical meta-lesson — **the proposals themselves contain misinformation while debunking misinformation**, which the critique caught brilliantly.

---

## The Defensible Case (What We Actually Know)

**Spider behavior makes regular ingestion implausible:**
- Spiders are vibration-sensitive organisms. A breathing, moving human registers as a threat. This is consistent with expert opinion (Rod Crawford, Burke Museum — a *verified* source).
- A sleeping human's mouth produces heat, moisture, and CO₂ — conditions spiders generally avoid.
- There is no ecological reason for spiders to approach a human mouth (no prey, no web anchors, no shelter value).

**No supporting evidence exists for the claim:**
- Zero peer-reviewed studies document spider ingestion during sleep.
- No credible scientific organization endorses the figure.
- The claim has been debunked by named, verifiable arachnologists.

**The claim is not literally impossible, but functionally zero.** A spider *could* theoretically end up in someone's mouth under unusual circumstances, but not as a systematic, average behavior across populations.

---

## The Origin Story Problem (What We Must Be Honest About)

The critique exposed the most important finding: **the commonly cited origin — "Lisa Holst, 1993, *PC Professional* magazine" — is itself unverified and possibly fabricated.** No copy of this article has ever been independently located. The name may be an anagram. Three of four proposals repeated some version of this story as established fact.

**What's defensible:** The myth was circulating by the mid-1990s via early internet channels. Its precise origin is unknown.

**What's not defensible:** Claiming with specificity that it was an intentional social experiment by a named author in a named publication.
