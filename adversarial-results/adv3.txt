**No, the statement is incorrect.** Albert Einstein was awarded the 1921 Nobel Prize in Physics **"for his services to Theoretical Physics, and especially for his discovery of the law of the photoelectric effect"** — not for his theory of relativity.

## Key Historical Facts (Verified)

1. **Official citation:** "for his services to Theoretical Physics, and especially for his discovery of the law of the photoelectric effect" (nobelprize.org)
2. **The prize was designated for 1921** but formally awarded in November 1922 under the Nobel "reserved prize" mechanism
3. **Einstein's 1905 paper** on the photoelectric effect proposed light quanta (photons), foundational to quantum mechanics
4. **Committee politics** were a significant factor — not merely insufficient evidence for relativity. The 1919 Eddington eclipse observations had broadly confirmed general relativity in the physics community
5. **Einstein's Nobel lecture discussed relativity**, not the photoelectric effect — an irony that underscores the complex relationship between the prize and Einstein's broader work

## Why This Misconception Persists

Einstein is culturally synonymous with relativity (E=mc²). The public narrative collapses "Einstein's greatest work" into "what he won the Nobel for," which are historically distinct. This is a textbook case of probabilistic association overriding specific factual records.
