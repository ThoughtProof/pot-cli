# Can a Cavity Heal Itself?

**No.** Once a cavity has physically breached the enamel surface (cavitation), it cannot heal itself. This is the consensus across all four models and is consistent with established clinical dentistry. The core reasoning is sound and uncontested.

---

## The Critical Distinction

**Remineralization ≠ Regeneration.** This category error (identified most clearly by Proposal 1) is the heart of the misconception.

- **Pre-cavitation (white spot lesions):** Subsurface mineral loss with intact enamel surface. Fluoride, saliva, and dietary changes can remineralize these lesions. This is real, evidence-based, and routinely practiced. Estimated 30–60% of early lesions can be arrested or reversed with professional fluoride protocols.
- **Cavitated lesions (actual cavity):** Physical structural collapse of enamel. Enamel is acellular post-eruption — no ameloblasts remain to deposit new tissue (Proposal 4's strongest contribution). Bacteria colonize the exposed dentin. No natural mechanism exists to regenerate lost tooth structure. Professional intervention is required.

---

## What All Models Missed: Tertiary Dentin and Arrested Caries

The Critic correctly identified two shared blind spots:

1. **Tertiary (reparative) dentin:** Odontoblasts can deposit reparative dentin in response to slow caries progression. This doesn't heal the cavity — the structural defect remains, bacteria persist — but it's a genuine biological defense that slows pulpal involvement. Ignoring it leaves a gap that cavity-healing proponents could exploit.

2. **Arrested cavitated caries:** Proposal 3 was the only one honest enough to acknowledge this. Cavitated lesions *can* arrest under extreme preventive conditions (radical dietary change, fluoride exposure, low-caries-risk environments). Arrest ≠ healing — the hole remains, the tooth is compromised — but this nuance matters for epistemic completeness. Proposals 1, 2, and 4 either denied or ignored this reality.

## Evidence Quality Warning

The Critic identified **significant fabrication risk in Proposal 2's citations**. Multiple references (Nyvad 1997 DOI, "Fujian study 2012," specific Cochrane parameters) could not be verified and show hallmarks of AI confabulation — precise DOIs, sample sizes, and statistics without confirmable sources. **Citation density is not evidence quality.** Proposal 2's numbers should not be treated as verified.

Proposals 1 and 3 also cite unverified statistics (e.g., "<30 microns" threshold, "0.5–1.5 mm/year" progression) but present them with less false precision.

---

## What Can Go Wrong

The asymmetric risk framing (Proposal 1's strongest argument) is decisive:

- **Believing cavities self-heal → delayed treatment → larger restorations, root canals ($1,500+ vs. $150–200 for a filling), extractions, infection, and in severe cases, systemic spread (cellulitis, sepsis)**
- **Believing cavities don't self-heal → at worst, slightly earlier intervention on a lesion that might have arrested**

The downside of the false belief is catastrophically worse than the downside of the true belief. This alone makes "cavities can self-heal" indefensible as general advice.
