# Synthesized Answer: Is Fluoride in Drinking Water Dangerous?

## Bottom Line

**No â€” community water fluoridation at 0.7 mg/L is safe for the vast majority of people and provides meaningful dental health benefits.** However, the evidence base is weaker than proponents typically claim, the strongest counter-evidence deserves honest engagement rather than dismissal, and the ethical dimension is legitimate.

## What the Evidence Actually Shows

**Benefits:** Water fluoridation at 0.7 mg/L reduces dental caries by approximately 25% in children. However â€” and this is critical â€” the Cochrane 2015 systematic review rated this evidence as **"very low quality"** by GRADE standards, with high risk of bias in most studies. Additionally, as fluoride toothpaste has become ubiquitous, the *marginal* benefit of water fluoridation has likely decreased from historical estimates. The commonly cited $1-saves-$20 figure (CDC, for communities >20,000) reflects real savings, particularly for low-income populations without regular dental access.

**Known risks at 0.7 mg/L:** Dental fluorosis (cosmetic white spots) affects roughly 25-40% of adolescents at some degree, with ~29% very mild, ~9% mild, and <1% moderate-to-severe per NHANES data. This is cosmetic, not a health threat. Acute poisoning at recommended water levels is physically impossible.

**The inconvenient counter-evidence all proposals underplayed:**

- **NTP 2024 Monograph:** The National Toxicology Program concluded with *moderate confidence* that fluoride exposure above 1.5 mg/L is associated with lower IQ in children. While 0.7 mg/L is below that threshold, the NTP did not conclusively establish a safe floor, and total daily fluoride intake from all sources (water, toothpaste, tea, food) can push individuals above 1.5 mg/L.

- **Green et al. 2019 (JAMA Pediatrics):** This study â€” conducted in **Canada**, not a high-fluoride developing country â€” found that a 1 mg/L increase in maternal urinary fluoride was associated with a 4.49-point lower IQ in boys. This is the single most relevant piece of counter-evidence because it studied a North American fluoridated population. It has methodological limitations but cannot be dismissed by claiming it only applies to China or India.

- **Cumulative exposure:** If you drink fluoridated water, use fluoride toothpaste, consume tea, and eat processed foods made with fluoridated water, total intake can approach or exceed 1.5 mg/L equivalent. No proposal adequately addressed this.
